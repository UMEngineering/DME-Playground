“If anyone deserves to play a fantasy computer game, it’s this kid.”

He chalks it up to his Catholic upbringing, but David Chesney can’t help but shoehorn some good into unexpected places. “I’ve always tried to build social context into the courses I teach,” said the University of Michigan computer science instructor.

So his section of ENGIN 100, a required course for all Michigan Engineering freshmen, is “Gaming for the Greater Good.”

Up until a few years ago, he was often inundated with algebra-teaching programs, as students would often build games around their favorite subject in high school.

Then, on a whim, he toured the special needs classroom in his daughter’s elementary school. There, he saw the technology set-up for a quadriplegic kid who had been injured in a car accident as a toddler.

“The device they had set up for him was a camera on top of his PC, and they’d put a metallic dot on his head that would move the cursor,” Chesney said. “That’s the main way he’d interface with his computer.”

Chesney was struck. “If anyone deserves to play a fantasy computer game, it’s this kid,” he remembers thinking.

He lifted a Nintendo controller that was setting on the table in front of him. “This is what today’s gaming interface looks like. I can barely use it.”

That tour would change the course of his course.
