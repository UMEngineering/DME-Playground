He’s only a college sophomore, and Steve is already saving lives — but not through the medical school. He works in U-M’s radiology lab, where they are designing a new form of cancer treatment that will more effectively target tumors while leaving uninfected organs unharmed.

As a first year, Steve wanted to choose some sort of project through the Undergraduate Research Opportunity Program. Of course, like most undergraduates fresh into college, he had a general idea of what he wanted to study but not many specifics. As a concentrator in nuclear engineering and physics, the radiology lab was an interesting and appropriate fit.

With a dentist father and nurse mother, Steve grew up around the field of medicine and knew that he might like to end up in that work area, too. “I’ve considered a career in medical physics research because it’s an active field in which I believe I could make a substantial contribution,” he explains. “I’m also considering a career in plasma physics research.” The lab was the perfect opportunity to practice working with engineering algorithms, models and data files, while also giving him experience with medical research — which he can use regardless of his career’s final destination.

If that sounds cutting-edge and complicated, it’s because it is. Every day is an opportunity to learn something new, which is exactly why the work appeals to Steve. “I feel like I’m helping others while learning at the same time,” he says, expressing just how cool it is that the research he’s doing today could become standard procedure for cancer patients worldwide.

Perhaps it’s surprising, given the heroic qualities of his work, that Steve is your average college student outside of the lab: He holds down three jobs, including one with U-M Dining Services; loves trying to figure out the movie “Inception” and was carrying around a tennis racket, eagerly looking forward to his game later that day.

Student by day, developing new cancer treatments by night. It’s all just a day in the life of Steve Exelby.
