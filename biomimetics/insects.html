Beetle whiter teeth → brighter paper

The Cyphochilus beetle has a face only a mother bug could love. But according to Michigan Engineering Professor David Kohn, it has the makings of a good dentist. "Cyphochilus has scales that are 10 times thinner than a human hair and whiter than most substances found in nature. Due to their structure, the scales scatter all colors of light simultaneously, which results in a brilliant white light. Man-made materials can produce the same whiteness, but they're at least 100 times thicker. By mimicking the structure of these scales, engineers envision new materials ranging from whiter teeth to brighter paper, or because of how thin the structure is, it could be added to existing materials to increase whiteness."

Termite mounds → passive cooling

Termites get pretty fussy about heating and cooling â€“ they like to chill out at around 87 degrees in their mounds. Jong-Jin Kim, a U-M associate professor of architecture, said that they do this with an ingenious system of vents. Cool air moves from the ground though holes in the bottom of the mound, forcing hot air out vents in the top. Termites open and close vents as needed to keep the temp so close to 87 degrees that it's creepy. "It's a good model," Kim said. "A building can automate natural ventilation by synchronous control of motorized windows at the upper and lower levels. Sensors that measure interior and exterior temperatures provide feedback that determines when to open or close the windows. Eastgate Shopping Centre in Zimbabwe does this."

Mosquito → pain-free hypodermic needles

A mosquito's poke is fairly painless. This oddity led medical researchers in Japan to do their own poking around. They found that a mosquito's proboscis is highly serrated and touches the nerves of the skin at fewer points than the smooth surface of a needle would. As the contact area decreased, so too did the pain. Researchers teamed up with engineers to develop a serrated hypodermic needle that, like a mosquito's proboscis, touches the skin at fewer places and is, according to patients, pretty painless.

Butterflies → near-zero power video displays for smart devices

Researchers said a collective "what the hell?" when they noticed something peculiar about the wings of certain butterflies: they have tiny scales and ridges and reflect light in a way that creates optical interference. That, in turn, produces iridescent colors that change as the viewing angle changes. Some super-geeks transformed that biological curiosity into a video display that uses near-zero power whenever the displayed image is static (a real battery-saver) and refreshes fast enough to handle video images. Perfect for smart hand-held devices.

Beetles → fire extinguishers, inhalers and fuel injectors

Bombardier beetles turn a deaf antenna to anyone who says "don't spit" because that's how they defend themselves. Julian Vincent, a professor of mechanical engineering and biomimetics at the University of Bath, explained that the beetles spit powerful jets of hot, caustic liquid. "The beetle's internal reaction chamber produces two liquids: hydrogen peroxide and phenol. An enzyme breaks down the peroxide, producing oxygen, water and heat. The oxygen expands, forces open the chamber's elastic exit valve and propels the peroxide. This happens about 500 times a second." The beetle's spitting prowess has inspired fire extinguishers that are more effective and use less water, more efficient inhalers, and fuel injectors that burn fuel more efficiently and reduce pollution.
